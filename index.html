How This Guide Works

This guide accompanies a GPX file containing 297 waypoints generated by a Python pipeline that pulled real geospatial data from OpenStreetMap and scored every point using peer-reviewed research on Eastern wild turkey habitat selection in southeastern longleaf pine ecosystems. Nothing in this file is a guess. Every waypoint derives from measured water proximity, verified timber density, confirmed habitat edge diversity, road distance, and land boundary data. Where the data was incomplete, the guide tells you so honestly.

The GPX imports directly into onX Hunt (GPX 1.1, 118KB, well under the 4MB/3000-waypoint limits). Each waypoint name is formatted as [CATEGORY-SCORE] — e.g., [STRUT-128] means a strutting zone candidate with a composite score of 128.

What this guide is

An educational companion that explains why each spot was chosen, what makes turkey habitat work in this specific ecosystem, and how to think about the data when you're standing in the woods at 5:30 AM.

What this guide is not

Legal advice. A guarantee. A substitute for scouting. Regulations change every year. Always verify seasons, permits, and boundaries at MyFWC.com before you go.

Understanding the Landscape

The region at a glance — what makes this ecosystem unique for turkey hunting.

The study area covers roughly 1,800 square miles of north Florida from the Apalachicola River east to the Aucilla River, and from the Gulf coast north to the Georgia line. This is the southern terminus of the Appalachian Highlands' influence on Florida — the last place you'll find meaningful elevation changes before the state flattens into peninsula. The terrain is subtle: 2-3 meters of elevation advantage here separates a roost ridge from a feeding flat. Don't think mountains. Think micro-topography.

Dominant Ecosystem

Fire-maintained longleaf pine (Pinus palustris) with wiregrass (Aristida stricta) understory. This is what turkeys evolved in. The open understory gives them sight lines for predator detection. The wiregrass holds the insects they eat. The scattered hardwood drains along creeks give them roosting corridors with mature cypress and water oak canopy.

The Karst Connection

The Woodville Karst Plain — porous limestone riddled with sinkholes, springs, and underground rivers. This matters for hunting because:

  • Springs and sinkholes are permanent water sources turkeys rely on year-round
  • Karst creates subtle elevation features (sinkhole rims, spring runs) that concentrate turkey activity
  • The aquifer connection means water features shown on maps actually exist and flow

Spring Season Climate (March 21 – April 26)

  • Morning temps: 55–68°F. Afternoons: 78–88°F.
  • Fog common near spring runs and river corridors until 8:00–9:00 AM
  • Thunderstorms build most afternoons by mid-April. Plan your hunts for morning.
  • 48–72 hours after rain, gobbling activity and movement increase measurably

The backbone of the study area. Over half a million acres of contiguous longleaf pine, cypress swamp, and hardwood drain. The forest runs an aggressive prescribed burn program — recently burned compartments (1-3 years post-fire) are turkey magnets.

Key Terrain

  • Wakulla District (south): Wet. Extensive cypress swamps, seepage slopes, sinkholes. The Sopchoppy River corridor has the best roosting habitat in the region.
  • Liberty District (north): Drier uplands with more pronounced sandhills. Better visibility, more open strut zones along powerline cuts.
  • Bradwell Bay Wilderness: 24,602 acres of roadless swamp. 7 of the top 13 waypoints cluster on its border (scored 119-132).
  • Mud Swamp/New River Wilderness: The top-scoring waypoint in the entire dataset ([BORDER-132]) sits on its edge.

Key access roads

FR 13 (Sopchoppy area), SR 267 (Leon/Wakulla boundary), FR 314/365 (Bradwell Bay perimeter). Forest roads are unpaved and many are seasonal. Check the MVUM at the ranger station. Cell service is nonexistent in the interior — download your onX maps offline.

How the Scoring Model Works

Every waypoint score is a composite of six habitat factors weighted by peer-reviewed research on Eastern wild turkey habitat selection.

Factor Weight What It Measures
Habitat Edge Density 25% Distinct habitat types within 200m. Research shows 79% of hens select home ranges with 3-4 cover types.
Water Proximity 20% Distance to nearest water. Peak at 150m. Springs and sinkholes get extra weight as reliable year-round sources.
Elevation Advantage 15% Height above surrounding terrain. Currently scored with a neutral placeholder (40/100) — USGS queries not yet run.
Cover Diversity 15% Distinct cover types within 300m including road/trail openings used as travel corridors.
Mature Timber 15% Natural woodland scores up to 100. Managed plantation caps at 60. Named waterways add 20 points.
Clearing Proximity 10% Distance to meadows, grasslands, powerline corridors. Must have adjacent timber for escape cover.

The Pressure Modifier

Raw habitat score gets multiplied by a pressure modifier that separates "good habitat" from "good hunting."

Road Distance Curve

0-100m from road: 0.50-0.58x (every hunter walks here) 200-400m: 0.72-0.92x (still easy access) 600-800m: 1.05-1.20x (PEAK — far enough, close enough) 1000-1500m: 1.18-0.98x (diminishing returns) 2000m+: 0.75-0.60x (too far to hunt effectively)

Bonus Multipliers

  • Wilderness Border: 1.25x — Birds on huntable land adjacent to non-huntable wilderness. The wilderness acts as a refuge.
  • Water Crossing: 1.15x — A creek or swamp between the road and the point. Most hunters won't cross it. You will.
  • No Trail: 1.10x — No marked trail within 300m means no foot traffic beyond hunters.

Example: Why [BORDER-132] is the top waypoint

Base habitat score: ~70 (solid but not exceptional). Pressure modifier: 1.88x — Road distance 782m (1.20x) × Mud Swamp Wilderness border (1.25x) × Water crossing (1.15x) × No trail (1.10x). Final: 70 × 1.88 = 132. The habitat isn't the best. The huntability is.

Waypoint Categories

Each waypoint is classified into a behavioral category that suggests how to hunt it.

STRUT

161 waypoints • 54%

A clearing or right-of-way with adjacent mature timber where gobblers are likely to display. Clearing proximity ≥ 65 AND mature timber ≥ 45.

How to hunt

  • Set up 50-80 yards inside the timber, facing the clearing
  • Gobblers enter clearings from timber at dawn and mid-morning (10:00-11:00 AM)
  • Call sparingly — in open areas, turkeys can pinpoint your location
  • Powerline corridors: set up where the powerline crosses a creek or changes direction

FEED

114 waypoints • 38%

Diverse habitat edge where turkeys forage throughout the day. High edge density and cover diversity — the 3-4 cover type locations research identifies as the strongest predictor of turkey presence.

How to hunt

  • All-day spots — turkeys move through unpredictably
  • Set up at the transition between two habitat types
  • Mid-morning (9:30-11:00 AM) is prime — gobblers cruise edges looking for hens
  • Run-and-gun: call, wait 20 minutes, move to the next one

BORDER

13 waypoints • 4%

Huntable land within 200m of a non-huntable protected area. The highest-scoring category overall — 7 of the top 10 waypoints are BORDER.

How to hunt

  • Pre-dawn setup is critical — 45 minutes before first light
  • Set up 50-100m inside the huntable zone, facing the boundary
  • Don't call aggressively toward the wilderness
  • One-chance events — if you spook the bird, it retreats to the safe zone

WATER

9 waypoints • 3%

Springs, sinkholes, or named creek confluences with adjacent timber. Data-confirmed features that won't move. During dry spells, every turkey within 2 miles visits these daily.

How to hunt

  • Turkeys visit mid-morning (9:00-11:00 AM) and late afternoon (3:00-5:00 PM)
  • Set up 60-80m from water, not on top of it
  • Sinkholes often have elevated rims — set up on the rim
  • Springs are thermal refugia during hot afternoons

ROOST & LISTEN (Locked)

These categories require measured USGS elevation data to distinguish ridges from flats. Run the script without --skip-elevation to unlock. ROOST sites are the highest-percentage turkey hunting tactic — if you know where a gobbler roosts, you know where he'll be at 6:15 AM tomorrow.

Quick Field Reads

  • Score > 110: Top-tier. Plan a hunt around this spot.
  • Score 95-110: Strong. Hunt it if convenient to your route.
  • Score 88-95: Solid backup or midday wandering points.
  • Pressure mod > 1.30: Hard to reach. That's the point.
  • Pressure mod < 1.00: Easy access = high pressure. Hunt early season only.
  • Water: 99+: Sweet spot. Close enough to attract turkeys, far enough to set up between them and the water.
  • Timber: 90+: Mature natural woodland. Listen for roosted gobblers.
  • Clearing: 90+: Adjacent to a confirmed opening. Face it.
  • Elevation adv: 40: Placeholder. Don't trust elevation-dependent conclusions.
  • Land: [UNVERIFIED]: Check on onX before hunting.

Planning a Hunt Day

  1. Pick your land unit based on quota permits, day of week, and drive distance.
  2. Filter waypoints by county/land in onX.
  3. Pick a STRUT or BORDER waypoint for pre-dawn. Arrive 45 min before sunrise.
  4. Pick 2-3 FEED waypoints within 1 mile as mid-morning fallbacks.
  5. Pick a WATER waypoint for midday setup.
  6. Check road distances. 1200m = 25-minute walk in the dark. Add 50% for swamp.

Turkey Behavior in This Ecosystem

What makes North Florida Eastern wild turkeys tick — and how they respond to pressure.

Roosting

  • Tall longleaf pine (60+ feet), slash pine, and pond cypress in wetland margins
  • Trees that emerge above the surrounding canopy for 360-degree predator scan
  • Typically 0.3-1.0 miles from permanent water
  • Individual gobblers maintain 4-8 traditional roost trees and rotate among them

Morning Routine

  • Flydown: 30-90 minutes after dawn (up to 120 min under pressure by Week 2)
  • Move 0.3-0.8 miles to primary feeding areas
  • First hour focused on insects in wiregrass, especially in recently burned areas
  • Gobble rate peaks during dawn chorus: 2-6 gobbles per minute
  • Under pressure: gobbling drops 40-70% within 24 hours of season opening

Strutting

  • Open understory with wiregrass <18 inches tall, 30-60% canopy coverage
  • Primary display: 5:30-7:30 AM. Secondary: 3:30-5:00 PM.
  • Mid-day strutting declines sharply in heat (>80°F) or post-rain
  • Multiple gobblers may use the same traditional display areas within 0.25 miles

Response to Pressure

This is the most important section. By the second weekend, every turkey within 200m of a road has been called at, shot at, or spooked. The birds that survive are the ones using harder-to-reach habitat.

TimeframeWhat Changes
Days 1-5 Gobbling drops 40-70%. Roost exit delays 60-120 min. Strut zone abandonment 60-80%. Response distance to calls increases to 100-200 yards.
Week 2-4 Activity shifts to mid-day (10 AM - 2 PM). Roosting in denser/more isolated timber. Random movement patterns replace traditional routes.
Late Season Dominant gobbler harvest permits subordinate males (age 1) to gobble. Yearling gobbles are higher-frequency, irregular. Remaining birds are patternable again.

Weather & Timing

Best conditions: 48-72 hours after rain. Warming trend after cold front (activity resumes within 8-12 hours). Falling barometric pressure. Fog — birds stay on roost longer, giving you more time to set up.

Worst conditions: Sustained cold (<45°F). High wind. Active rain — but they move immediately after it stops.

What the Data Doesn't Know

Honest limitations of this guide.

  1. No elevation data yet (affects 30% of the scoring model). The elevation advantage factor uses a neutral placeholder of 40/100. Running the USGS queries will reshuffle rankings.
  2. 132 waypoints (44%) have unverified land status. Always verify on onX Hunt's land ownership layer.
  3. OSM data quality varies. Apalachicola NF is well-mapped. Aucilla WMA has sparser coverage.
  4. No prescribed burn data. The single most useful dataset for turkey hunting in longleaf ecosystems isn't available in OSM. Call the ranger station.
  5. No real-time turkey population data. The model predicts good habitat, not where turkeys currently are. Scouting is still required.
  6. Regulations change annually. Check MyFWC.com before every hunt.

The Cheat Sheet

Quick reference by scenario.

"I have no quota permits and want to hunt tomorrow"

Apalachicola National Forest or Aucilla WMA. Both walk-on, no quota, daily access. Focus on STRUT and FEED waypoints in Liberty County (ANF) or Jefferson County (Aucilla). Bring license, turkey permit, and management area permit.

"I drew a St. Marks tag"

4 days (April 7-11). Pre-scout the Panacea or Wakulla Unit. Focus on the 5 WATER/BORDER waypoints plus the boundary between the refuge and Wakulla Springs State Park.

"I drew a Talquin/Wakulla/Joe Budd weekend tag"

Weekends only. Talquin: creek/lake edges and powerline corridors. Joe Budd: shooting hours end at 1 PM. Wakulla: sinkholes and McBride Slough corridor.

"It's Week 3 and I haven't heard a gobble"

Stop hunting easy-access STRUT points. Pivot to BORDER waypoints (wilderness edges) and high-pressure-modifier FEED points. Cross a creek. Walk past the 800m mark. Hunt mid-morning (10-11 AM) instead of dawn.

"I want the highest-probability spot in the dataset"

[BORDER-132] at 30.099°N, -84.915°W on the Mud Swamp/New River Wilderness border. Apalachicola NF, Liberty County. No quota. Walk-on. Bring waders — you're crossing water, 782m from the nearest road.

"I only have time for one scouting trip"

Drive FR 314/365 along the Bradwell Bay Wilderness perimeter. Stop at the BORDER waypoints. Owl-call at dusk — if a gobbler shock-gobbles, you've confirmed a roost. Mark it. Come back opening morning.
